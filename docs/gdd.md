


### Overview

Indigo is a game about solving procedurally generated mysteries. The game is inspired by a life-long love of the murder-mystery genre and its adjacent sub-genres.

### Objectives

- **Include the audience.** Indigo is a game about *solving* mysteries, not simply telling the audience about them. There are few things more disappointing than a mystery in which the audience doesn't have the same access to evidence as the protagonist. The player should be driving the investigation.

- **The player should feel clever** Again, indigo is about *solving* mysteries. The real balancing act is in generating mysteries that are interesting enough without being too difficult to be solved by a layman.

- **The stakes are real** Many detective games fall short when the narrative overrides the player's agency. In a standard mystery, there is *one* right answer and the player has no real opportunity to make mistakes since the game must shepherd the player toward the correct answer. A procedurally generated investigation does not have to hold the players hand in the same way.

- **The investigation is re-playable** Most mysteries are *one-and-done*. Once the proverbial cat is out of the bag, the audience knows whodunit and in most cases it just isn't very interesting to revisit (some talented writers craft narratives that are just as compelling on the second read, but that is beyond the scope of this game)

- **Lean on the detective's intuition** Fuzzy logic, and an investigator's gut are key to solving mysteries.

### What makes a good mystery?

- **A motivated cast of characters** Arguably one of the most important facets of a good mystery are the suspects. A detective is typically trying to establish means, motive, and opportunity. The intrigue very much hangs on the plausible motivation of each suspect.

- **A compelling twist** A *really* good mystery often involves a compelling twist. A good twist requires *most* evidence to point clearly at the wrong suspect until a crucial piece of evidence is uncovered that reveals the actual culprit. The most obvious twist is a frame-up, but there are many other twist archetypes.

### Generating a mystery

Treat suspects like bags of traits. Traits can be used to generate evidence, which can later be placed in the environment.

Suspects need overlapping motivations. 

Suspects need overlapping traits. To make an interesting mystery suspects can only be eliminated from the pool via a combination of evidence.

Some traits/motivations are public or private. Public traits are immediately obvious and difficult to hide. Private traits are internal and unobvious.

#### Archetypal Crimes

- Murder (Stick to this for the prototype)
- Theft

#### Archetypal Twists

- None
- Frame-up
- Two culprits

#### Archetypal Motivations

- Secrets (They knew something about me OR someone I care about)
- Greed   (They had something valuable I wanted)
- Revenge (They hurt me OR someone I care about)
- Justice (What they were doing was wrong)
